Statistics — Interview Questions

Descriptive Statistics 20 questions
Summaries, central tendency, dispersion
1
What is descriptive statistics?
Methods to summarize and present data (mean, median, mode, spread) without inference.
2
Difference between mean, median and mode?
Mean is arithmetic average, median is middle value, mode is most frequent value; median resists outliers.
3
What are variance and standard deviation?
Variance measures average squared deviation; SD is its square root, indicating spread in original units.
4
What are range and IQR?
Range = max-min; IQR = difference between 75th and 25th percentiles, robust spread measure.
5
What are skewness and kurtosis?
Skewness measures asymmetry; kurtosis measures tail heaviness or peakness relative to normal.
6
What is a percentile/quartile?
Percentiles indicate position in ordered data (e.g., 25th percentile = Q1), quartiles split data into 4 equal parts.
7
What is a boxplot?
Visual summary showing median, IQR, whiskers and potential outliers for a distribution.
8
What is a histogram?
Bar chart of binned data showing frequency distribution across intervals.
9
What is a density plot?
Smoothed estimate of the distribution (kernel density) useful for continuous data visualization.
10
What is a frequency table?
Tabulation of counts or proportions for categorical or binned numerical data.
11
What is coefficient of variation?
Ratio of SD to mean, useful to compare relative variability across scales.
12
How detect outliers (IQR, z-score)?
IQR method flags points beyond 1.5×IQR; z-score flags points beyond chosen sigma thresholds.
13
What is winsorization?
Replace extreme values with nearest non-extreme quantile to reduce impact of outliers.
14
What summary measures are best for skewed data?
Median and IQR are preferred as they are robust to skew and outliers.
15
How summarize bivariate data?
Use contingency tables, scatter plots, correlation, and cross-tabulations for relationships.
16
Standardization vs normalization?
Standardization rescales to mean 0 SD 1; normalization rescales to a min–max range (e.g., 0–1).
17
What is Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)?
Initial data investigation using visuals and summaries to find patterns and data issues.
18
How inspect missing data patterns?
Use missingness maps, counts by variable, and check for patterns related to other features.
19
What is data profiling?
Automated summarization of data types, ranges, missingness, and anomalies for dataset health checks.
20
Visualization best practices?
Choose clear charts, label axes, use appropriate scales, avoid misleading representations.
Probability 20 questions
Basic probability rules and concepts
21
What is probability?
A number between 0 and 1 quantifying the likelihood of an event under a model.
22
What is the addition rule?
P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B); for mutually exclusive events, intersection is zero.
23
What is the multiplication rule?
P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B|A); if independent, P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B).
24
What is conditional probability?
Probability of event A given B occurred: P(A|B)=P(A∩B)/P(B).
25
State Bayes' theorem in words.
It updates the probability of a hypothesis given new evidence using prior and likelihood.
26
What does independence mean?
Events A and B are independent if P(A|B)=P(A), equivalently P(A∩B)=P(A)P(B).
27
Mutually exclusive vs independent?
Mutually exclusive cannot co-occur; independent can co-occur and their probabilities multiply.
28
What is the Law of Large Numbers?
Sample averages converge to expected value as sample size grows.
29
What are Kolmogorov's axioms?
Non-negativity, normalization (P(Ω)=1), and additivity for disjoint events.
30
What is the complement rule?
P(A^c)=1−P(A): probability event does not occur equals 1 minus probability it does.
31
When use Bayes in practice?
When updating probabilities with new evidence or combining prior knowledge with data.
32
Discrete vs continuous distributions?
Discrete assigns probabilities to outcomes; continuous uses densities, probabilities for intervals.
33
Joint, marginal and conditional distributions?
Joint = P(X,Y); marginal = sum/integrate joint over one variable; conditional = joint/marginal.
34
What is expected value?
Weighted average of a random variable's possible values using their probabilities.
35
What is LOTUS (law of unconscious statistician)?
Compute E[g(X)] by summing/integrating g(x) against the distribution of X without transforming variable.
36
What is an MGF?
Moment generating function uniquely encodes moments; derivatives at zero give moments.
37
CDF vs PDF vs PMF?
CDF gives cumulative prob; PDF density for continuous; PMF probabilities for discrete outcomes.
38
Law of total probability?
Decompose P(A) via partition B_i: P(A)=Σ P(A|B_i)P(B_i).
39
What is the Monty Hall/Bertrand paradox idea?
They illustrate how conditional probability and problem framing change intuitive answers.
40
What is a random experiment?
Process with uncertain outcomes (trial) defined by a sample space and probability law.
Random Variables & Distributions 20 questions
Common distributions and properties
41
What is a random variable?
Mapping from outcomes to numerical values; can be discrete or continuous.
42
What is Bernoulli distribution?
Single-trial discrete distribution with success probability p (0 or 1 outcomes).
43
What is Binomial distribution?
Sum of independent Bernoulli trials; models number of successes in n trials.
44
What is Poisson distribution?
Models counts of rare events in fixed interval with rate λ, approximates binomial for small p.
45
What is the uniform distribution?
All outcomes in the domain equally likely (discrete or continuous variants).
46
Why is the normal distribution important?
Central limit theorem makes many sums/averages approximately normal; analytically convenient.
47
What is the exponential distribution?
Memoryless continuous distribution modeling waiting times with rate λ.
48
What is the gamma distribution?
Flexible family for positive variables; generalizes exponential (sum of exponentials).
49
What is the beta distribution?
Distribution on [0,1] parameterized by shape parameters, useful as conjugate prior for Bernoulli/Binomial.
50
What is Student's t-distribution?
Heavy-tailed distribution used for small-sample inference about means; approaches normal as df increases.
51
What is chi-square distribution?
Distribution of sum of squared standard normals; used in variance tests and contingency tables.
52
What is F-distribution?
Ratio of scaled chi-square variables; used in ANOVA and variance ratio tests.
53
What is geometric distribution?
Models number of trials to first success in independent Bernoulli trials (memoryless discrete).
54
What is negative binomial distribution?
Models counts until r-th success, generalizes geometric and models overdispersed count data.
55
What is the multinomial distribution?
Multivariate generalization of binomial for counts across multiple categories.
56
Covariance vs correlation?
Covariance measures joint variability; correlation scales covariance to [−1,1] for comparison.
57
What are moments?
Expectations of powers: first moment mean, second central moment variance, etc., describe distribution shape.
58
What is a characteristic function?
Fourier transform of the distribution; uniquely determines distribution and helps with distribution sums.
59
What is a mixture distribution?
A weighted combination of component distributions modeling heterogeneity or multimodality.
60
What is empirical distribution function (EDF)?
Step function estimating CDF from sample data; basis for nonparametric inference.
Inferential Statistics 20 questions
Estimation, CI, hypothesis framework
61
What is statistical inference?
Drawing conclusions about population parameters from sample data using probabilistic models.
62
Point estimation vs interval estimation?
Point estimates give a single value; interval estimates give a range with confidence level.
63
Interpretation of a 95% confidence interval?
If repeated samples taken, ~95% of such intervals would contain the true parameter (frequentist view).
64
What is standard error?
Standard deviation of a sampling distribution of an estimator (e.g., mean's SE = σ/√n).
65
Why is CLT useful in inference?
Allows approximate normality of sample means for constructing CIs and tests even if data non-normal.
66
What is Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)?
Choose parameters maximizing the likelihood of observing the given data under the model.
67
Desirable properties of estimators?
Unbiasedness, consistency, efficiency (low variance), and sufficiency are common criteria.
68
What is consistency of an estimator?
Estimator converges in probability to the true parameter as sample size increases.
69
Bias–variance tradeoff in estimation?
Tradeoff where lower bias often increases variance and vice versa; aim to minimize overall error.
70
What is method of moments?
Estimate parameters by equating sample moments to theoretical moments of distribution.
71
What is a sufficient statistic?
Statistic summarizing all information about parameter present in the sample (no loss of info).
72
What is ancillary statistic?
Statistic whose distribution does not depend on the parameter; can help conditional inference.
73
What is Cramér–Rao lower bound?
Lower bound on variance of unbiased estimators; a measure of estimator efficiency.
74
What is the likelihood ratio test?
Compare max likelihoods under null and alternative hypotheses; often asymptotically χ²-distributed.
75
What is the bootstrap?
Resampling with replacement to estimate sampling distributions and CIs nonparametrically.
76
What is the jackknife?
Leave-one-out resampling method for estimating bias and variance of estimators.
77
Parametric vs nonparametric inference?
Parametric assumes distributional form; nonparametric makes fewer assumptions and is more flexible.
78
What is asymptotic normality?
Distributions of many estimators become approximately normal as sample size grows.
79
What is profile likelihood?
Maximize likelihood over nuisance parameters to focus on parameter of interest for CI and tests.
80
What is empirical Bayes?
Estimate prior from data and then apply Bayes, blending frequentist and Bayesian ideas.
Hypothesis Testing 20 questions
Tests, p-values, errors, power
81
What are the steps in hypothesis testing?
Set H0/H1, choose α, compute test statistic, find p-value or critical region, conclude.
82
What is a p-value?
Probability of data as extreme or more so under the null hypothesis; not the probability null is true.
83
Type I vs Type II error?
Type I: reject true null; Type II: fail to reject false null (false positive vs false negative).
84
How choose significance level α?
Based on tolerance for false positives and study context; common values 0.05 or 0.01.
85
One-tailed vs two-tailed tests?
One-tailed tests directionally test H1; two-tailed test checks for any difference in either direction.
86
What is power of a test?
Probability to reject the null when alternative true (1−β), depends on effect size and sample size.
87
What is effect size?
Quantifies magnitude of phenomenon; larger effect sizes make detection easier and increase power.
88
How adjust for multiple comparisons?
Use Bonferroni, Holm, FDR/Benjamini–Hochberg to control family-wise or false discovery rates.
89
Paired vs unpaired tests?
Paired for related samples (before/after); unpaired for independent groups.
90
What is t-test?
Test comparing means using t-distribution when population SD unknown and sample sizes small.
91
What is ANOVA?
Analysis of variance compares means across multiple groups using between- and within-group variability.
92
What is chi-square test used for?
Test independence of categorical variables or goodness-of-fit to expected counts.
93
When use nonparametric tests?
When assumptions like normality or equal variances are violated; examples: Mann–Whitney, Kruskal–Wallis.
94
Can bootstrap be used for hypothesis testing?
Yes—use resampling to estimate null distributions or compute p-values nonparametrically.
95
What is a permutation test?
Assess significance by shuffling labels to generate null distribution for test statistic.
96
Statistical significance vs practical significance?
Statistical relates to p-values; practical significance concerns real-world effect size and relevance.
97
What is sequential testing?
Testing procedure that allows interim looks and early stopping while controlling error rates.
98
How test a single proportion?
Use z-test for proportions or exact binomial tests when sample sizes small.
99
What is power analysis?
Compute sample size or power given effect size and α to design studies with adequate detection ability.
100
What are robust tests?
Tests less sensitive to assumption violations, e.g., using ranks or trimmed means.
Correlation & Regression 20 questions
Linear models, assumptions, diagnostics
101
What is correlation?
Measure of linear association between two variables, commonly Pearson correlation.
102
Pearson vs Spearman correlation?
Pearson measures linear relation; Spearman measures monotonic relation using ranks.
103
What is regression analysis?
Model relationship between predictors and a response, often for prediction or inference.
104
Key OLS assumptions?
Linearity, independence, homoscedasticity, no perfect multicollinearity, normally distributed errors for inference.
105
What is R-squared?
Proportion of variance in response explained by predictors; can be misleading for model comparison.
106
Why use adjusted R-squared?
Penalizes adding predictors; gives a better measure for model comparison with different feature counts.
107
How detect multicollinearity?
Check VIF, condition number, or unstable coefficient estimates and high pairwise correlations.
108
How detect heteroscedasticity?
Residual plots, Breusch–Pagan or White tests indicate non-constant error variance.
109
Why inspect residuals?
To check model fit, detect nonlinearity, heteroscedasticity, outliers and influential points.
110
What are influential observations? (Cook's distance)
Points that disproportionately affect model estimates; Cook’s distance identifies them for review.
111
Lasso vs Ridge regression?
Lasso (L1) produces sparse coefficients; Ridge (L2) shrinks coefficients smoothly; Elastic Net mixes both.
112
What is a generalized linear model (GLM)?
Framework linking linear predictors to response via a link function and distribution from exponential family.
113
Common GLM link functions?
Logit for binomial, log for Poisson, identity for Gaussian, complementary log-log in some cases.
114
What is robust regression?
Methods less sensitive to outliers (e.g., Huber loss, quantile regression) for reliable estimates.
115
What is quantile regression?
Models conditional quantiles (e.g., median) instead of the mean for distributional insights.
116
How handle nonlinear relationships?
Use polynomial features, splines, GAMs, or transform variables to capture nonlinearity.
117
What are interaction effects?
When effect of one predictor depends on value of another; include product terms to model them.
118
How include categorical predictors?
One-hot, effect coding, target encoding or embeddings depending on model and cardinality.
119
What are AIC and BIC?
Information criteria balancing fit and complexity for model selection; BIC penalizes complexity more.
120
How use cross-validation in regression?
Assess predictive performance and tune hyperparameters (e.g., regularization strength) via CV.
Sampling & Estimation 20 questions
Sampling methods, estimators, bias/variance
121
Common sampling methods?
Simple random, stratified, cluster, systematic, and convenience sampling with trade-offs in bias and cost.
122
What is sampling bias?
When sample is not representative of population due to selection processes, causing biased inference.
123
What is a sampling distribution?
Distribution of a statistic (e.g., sample mean) over repeated samples from the population.
124
Role of CLT in sampling?
Justifies approximate normality of sample means for large n, enabling approximate CIs and tests.
125
How calculate sample size?
Based on desired power, effect size, significance level, and variance or pilot estimates.
126
Bias vs variance of an estimator?
Bias = systematic error; variance = sampling variability; both contribute to MSE.
127
What is an unbiased estimator?
Its expected value equals the true parameter across repeated samples.
128
What does consistency mean for estimators?
Estimator converges in probability to the true parameter as sample size → ∞.
129
What is estimator efficiency?
An efficient estimator has minimal variance among unbiased estimators (often close to Cramér–Rao bound).
130
Examples of biased estimators?
Sample variance using 1/n is biased; using 1/(n−1) yields unbiased estimator.
131
Bootstrap sampling basics?
Resample with replacement to simulate sampling distribution when analytic forms unavailable.
132
Why use stratified sampling?
Improves representativeness and precision by sampling within homogeneous subgroups.
133
When use cluster sampling?
When population naturally groups and resources limit sampling; may increase variance vs SRS.
134
What is systematic sampling?
Select every k-th item from an ordered list; simple but may introduce periodicity bias.
135
What is response bias?
Bias from non-response or inaccurate responses affecting sample representativeness.
136
What is finite population correction (FPC)?
Adjustment to variance when sampling a substantial fraction of finite population (reduces SE).
137
What is post-stratification?
Adjust sample weights after data collection to match known population margins and reduce bias.
138
What is importance sampling?
Use a proposal distribution to estimate expectations more efficiently for rare events.
139
Types of sampling error?
Sampling error (due to chance) and nonsampling errors (measurement, coverage, response).
140
What is design effect?
Ratio of variance under actual design to variance under simple random sampling, accounts for clustering/weights.
Bayesian Statistics 20 questions
Priors, posteriors, Bayesian inference
141
What is Bayesian inference?
Update prior beliefs with data likelihood to obtain posterior distribution for parameters.
142
Types of priors (informative, noninformative)?
Informative priors encode domain knowledge; noninformative are vague to let data dominate.
143
What is a conjugate prior?
Prior leading to posterior in same family as prior, simplifying analytic updates (e.g., Beta-Binomial).
144
What is the posterior predictive distribution?
Predictive distribution for future data integrating over posterior parameter uncertainty.
145
What is MCMC?
Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods sample from complex posteriors when analytic forms unavailable.
146
What is Gibbs sampling?
MCMC technique sampling each parameter conditional on others iteratively to approximate joint posterior.
147
What is Metropolis–Hastings?
MCMC algorithm proposing moves and accepting with probability based on ratio of target densities.
148
Bayesian vs frequentist interpretation?
Bayesian treats parameters as random with priors; frequentist uses fixed parameters and sampling ideas.
149
What is Bayes factor?
Ratio of marginal likelihoods for model comparison quantifying evidence in data for one model vs another.
150
Credible interval vs confidence interval?
Credible interval: Bayesian probability interval for parameter; confidence interval: frequentist coverage property.
151
What is Empirical Bayes?
Estimate prior parameters from data and then perform Bayesian updating; pragmatic compromise approach.
152
What are hierarchical Bayesian models?
Models with parameters drawn from higher-level distributions allowing pooled estimation across groups.
153
What is prior sensitivity?
Assessment of how inference changes with different prior choices; important when data sparse.
154
What is variational inference?
Approximate posterior with tractable family by optimizing divergence (faster than MCMC for large problems).
155
What is posterior predictive checking?
Compare simulated data from posterior predictive to observed data to assess model fit.
156
Give a conjugate prior example.
Beta prior with Binomial likelihood yields Beta posterior for success probability.
157
Posterior vs posterior predictive?
Posterior describes parameters; posterior predictive integrates over posterior to predict new data.
158
What is Bayesian model averaging?
Combine predictions from multiple models weighted by posterior model probabilities to account for model uncertainty.
159
What is Bayesian decision theory?
Decision-making framework minimizing expected loss under posterior uncertainty.
160
How use priors in ML models?
Regularization can be seen as priors; incorporate domain knowledge or structure via priors.
Time Series 20 questions
Forecasting basics, stationarity, ARIMA
161
What is a time series?
Sequence of observations indexed by time, often exhibiting trend and seasonality.
162
What is stationarity?
Statistical properties (mean, variance) are time-invariant; crucial for many models.
163
What is white noise?
Sequence of uncorrelated random variables with zero mean and constant variance.
164
What is autocorrelation?
Correlation of a series with its own lagged values, used with ACF/PACF plots.
165
ACF vs PACF?
ACF shows correlations at lags; PACF shows partial correlations controlling for intermediate lags.
166
What is differencing?
Subtract previous value(s) to remove trend/seasonality and achieve stationarity.
167
What is ARIMA (p,d,q)?
ARIMA models autoregression (p), differencing (d), and moving average (q) for forecasting.
168
What is SARIMA?
Seasonal ARIMA adds seasonal AR/MA/differencing terms for periodic patterns.
169
What is exponential smoothing?
Weighted averaging of past observations with exponentially decaying weights (e.g., Holt-Winters).
170
Common forecasting metrics?
MAE, RMSE, MAPE, and mean absolute scaled error (MASE) depending on scale/seasonality.
171
What is a unit root?
Characteristic of nonstationary series (random walk); presence tested by Dickey–Fuller tests.
172
How detect seasonality?
Inspect periodic patterns in plots, ACF peaks at seasonal lags, or spectral analysis.
173
What is cointegration?
Long-run equilibrium relationship between nonstationary series whose linear combination is stationary.
174
What is Granger causality?
Predictive causality test: one series helps forecast another beyond its own history.
175
What are state-space models?
Models representing observed series via unobserved states evolving over time (e.g., Kalman filter).
176
What is Kalman filter?
Recursive algorithm to estimate hidden states in linear Gaussian state-space models.
177
Why combine forecasts?
Ensemble of forecasts often improves accuracy and robustness across methods.
178
How perform time-series cross-validation?
Use forward-chaining (rolling origin) splits preserving temporal order to avoid leakage.
179
How remove trend and seasonality?
Use differencing, decomposition (additive/multiplicative), or regression on time/seasonal dummies.
180
How detect anomalies in time series?
Use statistical thresholds, residual-based detection, seasonal decomposition, or specialized models.
Statistical Modeling for ML 20 questions
Modeling assumptions, evaluation, experiment design
181
Why check model assumptions?
Violations can invalidate inference or degrade predictive performance, so diagnostics are essential.
182
What is model misspecification?
When the assumed model form doesn't match data-generating process, leading to biased/poor results.
183
Statistical view of regularization?
Regularization penalizes complexity and can be interpreted as imposing priors in a Bayesian viewpoint.
184
Why use cross-validation in ML?
Estimate generalization error and select/tune models while avoiding overfitting to a single split.
185
Why is interpretability important?
It builds trust, helps debugging, ensures fairness, and satisfies regulatory needs.
186
What is causal inference vs correlation?
Causal inference aims to estimate effect of interventions, requiring design/assumptions beyond association.
187
What is a confounder?
A variable influencing both treatment and outcome that can bias causal estimates if unaccounted for.
188
Why randomize experiments?
Randomization balances observed and unobserved confounders, enabling unbiased causal estimates.
189
What is propensity score matching?
Match units with similar treatment probabilities to reduce confounding in observational studies.
190
Key elements of A/B testing?
Random assignment, clear metric, sufficient sample size, and correct statistical analysis for inference.
191
How control error in sequential A/B tests?
Use alpha-spending, sequential tests, or Bayesian approaches to maintain error rates with interim looks.
192
What are causal graphs (DAGs)?
Directed acyclic graphs representing causal relationships and guiding identification strategies.
193
What is an instrumental variable (IV)?
IV influences treatment but not outcome directly, enabling causal identification under assumptions.
194
What is difference-in-differences?
Compare pre-post differences between treated and control groups to estimate causal effects under parallel trends.
195
What is synthetic control method?
Construct weighted combination of controls to form a counterfactual for treated unit in comparative case studies.
196
How does regularization trade bias and variance?
Regularization increases bias slightly but reduces variance, often lowering overall error.
197
Why use ensemble methods?
Combine models to reduce variance and improve predictions by leveraging diverse model strengths.
198
What is probability calibration?
Adjust predicted probabilities so they match observed frequencies (Platt scaling, isotonic regression).
199
Principles of experimental design?
Randomization, control, replication, blocking, and clear outcome measurement for valid inference.
200
How improve statistical literacy?
Practice real data analysis, study concepts, simulate experiments, and communicate results clearly.
